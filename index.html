Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)

Description

The Thematic apperception test (TAT) is a type of projective test that involves describing ambiguous scenes to learn more about a person's emotions, motivations, and personality. It was developed during the 1930s by Henry Murray and Christiana Morgan. The original test and its modifications (like MITT) sometimes used for lie detection purposes.

Handbooks

Two classical handbooks on Thematic Apperception Test. The first is "The thematic apperception test; the theory and technique of interpretation" by Silvan Solomon Tomkins and Elizabeth J. Tomkins. Publisher: Grune & Stratton, publication date - 1947.

The second is "The Thematic Apperception Test, the Children's Apperception Test, and the Senior Apperception Technique in Clinical Use" by Leopold Bellak. Publisher: Grune & Stratton, publication date - 1975.

The original TAT Manual

The Thematic Apperception Test and its pictures evolved during the 1930s and early 1940s. The process culminated in the version published by Henry A. Murray and the Staff of the Harvard Psychological Clinic in 1943. The original TAT Manual, pudbished in 1943 in Cambridge, MA.
