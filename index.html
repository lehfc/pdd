News & Views

A very valuable newsletter that was published by polygraph examiner James R. Wygant and intended for Oregon polygraph examiners. Although the newsletter began with a local focus, it grew to include subscribers in nearly every state and a few foreign countries. For a time there was even a column about equipment tips. When instrumentation changed from analog to digital, that column was dropped and one was added summarizing polygraph news stories from the internet. This is a single 261p. PDF file, a compilation of every issue of Polygraph News & Views from the first one, November, 1993, through the year 2005.

Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)

Handbooks Manual Pictures

Description

The Thematic apperception test (TAT) is a type of projective test that involves describing ambiguous scenes to learn more about a person's emotions, motivations, and personality. It was developed during the 1930s by Henry Murray and Christiana Morgan.

The TAT is a projective examination where an individual is shown vague sketches and asked to make up stories about them. The stories should include what happened prior to the scene in the sketch, what is happening in the sketch, and an ending. The original test and its modifications (like MITT) sometimes used for lie detection purposes.

Handbooks

We have two classical handbooks on Thematic Apperception Test. The first is "The thematic apperception test; the theory and technique of interpretation" by Silvan Solomon Tomkins and Elizabeth J. Tomkins. Publisher: Grune & Stratton, publication date - 1947.

The second is "The Thematic Apperception Test, the Children's Apperception Test, and the Senior Apperception Technique in Clinical Use" by Leopold Bellak. Publisher: Grune & Stratton, publication date - 1975.

TAT Manual

The Thematic Apperception Test and its pictures evolved during the 1930s and early 1940s. The process culminated in the version published by Henry A. Murray and the Staff of the Harvard Psychological Clinic in 1943. In our collection, we have the original TAT Manual, pudbished in 1943 in Cambridge, MA.
